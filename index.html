3 · Why a stacked grid (instead of running bond)

You asked for no cut pavers, and that decides the pattern. A half-offset running bond looks great but forces a half-paver cut at both ends of every other row — unavoidable cuts. A stacked grid (“jack-on-jack”), where every paver lines up in a clean square gridiron, needs zero cuts: the field is simply 24 columns × 7 rows of whole 12" squares. It also happens to total exactly one 168-paver pallet. Lay the courses square to the walkway and keep consistent joints; the brick walkway gives you a straight front line to start from.

6 · Build steps

  1. Clear & rough-gradePull the brush, trash cans, wheelbarrow and old debris. Strip sod/weeds and dig the strip down so the finished pavers sit flush with (or just above) the brick walkway. Pitch the grade to fall about 1" per 8 ft away from the fence toward the yard/walkway for drainage.
  2. Compact the soilRake smooth, remove rocks/roots, then tamp firmly (a hand tamp is fine for this size, or rent a plate compactor). A flat, solid sub-grade is what makes PaverBase work.
  3. Lay separation / weed fabricRoll the 4’-wide barrier over the compacted subgrade, overlapping seams ~4" and running it a few inches up the fence side. Pin it with the included U-shaped pegs. It blocks weeds and keeps the Class 5 from mixing into the soil below.
  4. Spread & compact the Class 5 baseSpread 4" of Class 5 over the fabric in two ~2" lifts, grading each to pitch ~1" per 8 ft away from the fence. Plate-compact each lift firm (Class 5 settles ~20–25%, so it goes down looser than 4"). This is your drainage and frost layer.
  5. Screed a thin leveling course (optional)If the compacted Class 5 isn’t glass-flat, screed ~1/2" of coarse sand over it and level it — the Brock panels need a smooth plane to sit on.
  6. Install Brock PaverBase panelsLay the 5 sq ft panels edge-to-edge across the field, staggering the end seams like bricks. Cut the panels to fit with a utility knife (panels can be cut freely — only the pavers stay whole).
  7. Set the ProFlex edgingRun edging along the fence side and both ends, snug to where the pavers finish. Drive the 8" spikes through the tabs at each end and every 18" (4 per 6-ft piece). The brick walkway restrains the front edge.
  8. Lay the pavers (stacked grid — no cuts)Start at the straight walkway edge and lay 24 whole pavers per row, square to the walkway, keeping every joint lined up in a grid. Work back 7 rows to 84". Tap each level with a rubber mallet. No paver gets cut — the leftover strip at the fence and the 3" at the stair end stay open for fill.
  9. Sweep in EZ SandWith pavers dry, sweep the gray polymeric sand into all joints until full. Sweep every bit of excess off the paver faces, then tamp so sand settles and top off thin joints.
  10. Activate the sandMist with water per the bag directions (a light shower, not a flood) to set the polymer. Let it cure — avoid foot traffic and rain for the time stated on the bag (usually 24 hrs).
  11. Fill the fence gap & place firepitBackfill the 10–15" fence-side strip (and the 3" stair-end gap) with gravel or mulch. Set the firepit toward the front. Done.

7 · Fire safety — please read

This is a narrow strip against a wood fence with string lights and dry brush nearby. The no-cut layout actually helps a little — the pad stops 84" deep and the gravel gap pushes usable surface ~10"+ off the fence — but the fire will still be far closer to combustibles than the usual 10–25 ft guideline. To reduce risk:

  • Keep the firepit toward the walkway (front) side to maximize fence clearance — the suggested spot reflects this.
  • Use a spark screen, keep flames modest, and never leave it unattended.
  • Clear the dry brush, leaves, and weeds along the fence before first use, and keep water or an extinguisher nearby.
  • Check your municipality’s rules on open fires and minimum distances from fences/structures — some areas require more clearance than this strip provides.
